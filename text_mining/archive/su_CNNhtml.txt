el__leafmedia--sourced-paragraph"><p class="zn-body__paragraph"><cite class="el-editorial-source">Washington (CNN)</cite>President Barack Obama made a <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2015/01/17/politics/obama-state-of-the-union-taxes/index.html" target="_blank">feisty pitch for economic populism</a> in his penultimate State of the Union Tuesday night, touting the improving economy on his watch and making the case that government has a role in ensuring equal opportunity for all.</p></div><p class="zn-body__paragraph">Obama began his speech at 9:10 p.m. in front of a joint session of the House and Senate, declaring that "the shadow of crisis has passed, and the state of the union is strong."</p><p class="zn-body__paragraph">"At this moment -- with a growing economy, shrinking deficits, bustling industry, and booming energy production -- we have risen from recession freer to write our own future than any other nation on Earth," he said.  </p><p class="zn-body__paragraph">"It's now up to us to choose who we want to be over the next fifteen years, and for decades to come," he said. "Will we accept an economy where only a few of us do spectacularly well? Or will we commit ourselves to an economy that generates rising incomes and chances for everyone who makes the effort?"</p><p class="zn-body__paragraph">Obama argued that a raft of new policy proposals — which include an ambitious plan to raise taxes on wealthy Americans and financial institutions to pay for progressive priorities — favor the middle class, and that "middle-class economics works."</p><p class="zn-body__paragraph">And he laid down the gauntlet to Republicans: They'll "continue to work, as long as politics don't get in the way."</p><p class="zn-body__paragraph">It's a defiant speech for the President, now freed from the shackles of vulnerable red-state Democrats up for re-election and looking to burnish his legacy in his final two years.</p><div class="ad ad--epic ad--mobile" data-ad-text="show"><div id="ad_rect_btf_02" class="ad-ad_rect_btf_02"></div></div><ul class="cn cn-list-hierarchical-xs cn--idx-8"></ul><p class="zn-body__paragraph"><a href="http://www.cnn.com/2015/01/20/politics/state-of-the-union-2015-transcript-full-text/index.html" target="_blank">Read the full transcript of President Obama's address</a></p><p class="zn-body__paragraph">Instead of granting Republicans their wish for a realistic compromiser-in-chief chastened by a midterm election drubbing, Obama is attempting to set the terms of an economic debate that could continue into the 2016 race to replace him in the Oval Office. </p><p class="zn-body__paragraph">He called for cooperation, reprising the theme that there isn't a "red America" or "blue America" from the 2004 Democratic National Convention speech that shot Obama into stardom. And he flashed a renewed confidence, going off-script after touting the improving economy to say, "This is good news, people" -- and followed it with a wink.</p><p class="zn-body__paragraph">Later, when Obama said he had no campaigns left to run and some Republicans sarcastically clapped, the President shot back: "I know, 'cause I won both of them."</p><div class="el__gallery--standard
