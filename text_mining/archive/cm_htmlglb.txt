							<!----><p></p><p>NEW YORK (AP) &mdash; Tens of thousands of activists walked through Manhattan on Sunday, warning that climate change is destroying the Earth &mdash; in stride with demonstrators around the world who urged policymakers to take quick action.</p><p>Starting along Central Park West, most came on foot, others with bicycles and walkers, and some even in wheelchairs. Many wore costumes and marched to drumbeats. One woman played the accordion.</p><p class="skip-nav article-more">
	<a href="#skip-target1">Continue reading below</a>
</p>

									<p class="bg-related__description">Activists mobilized in cities across the globe Sunday for marches against climate change, with one of the biggest planned for New York</p>

	<p><a id="skip-target-related-box"></a></p>

<p>Organizers said more than 100,000 marched in New York, including actors Mark Ruffalo and Evangeline Lilly. They were joined in midtown Manhattan by United Nations Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon, former Vice President Al Gore and New York City Mayor Bill de Blasio.</p><p>On Tuesday, more than 120 world leaders will convene for the United Nations Climate Summit aimed at galvanizing political will for a new global climate treaty by the end of 2015.</p><p>&lsquo;&lsquo;My sense is the energy you see on the streets, the numbers that have amassed here and in other cities around the world, show that something bigger is going on, and this U.N. summit will be one of the ones where we look back and say it was a difference maker,&rsquo;&rsquo; de Blasio said.</p><p>Ban agreed.</p><p>&lsquo;&lsquo;Climate change is a defining issue of our time and there is no time to lose,&rsquo;&rsquo; he said. &lsquo;&lsquo;There is no Plan B because we do not have planet B. We have to work and galvanize our action.&rsquo;&rsquo;</p><p>The New York march was one of a series of events held around the world to raise awareness about climate change.</p><p>In London, organizers said 40,000 marchers participated, while a small gathering in Cairo featured a huge art piece representing wind and solar energy. In Rio de Janeiro, marchers at Ipanema Beach had green hearts painted on their faces.</p><p>Celebrities in London including actress Emma Thompson and musician Peter Gabriel joined thousands of people crossing the capital&rsquo;s center, chanting: &lsquo;&lsquo;What do we want? Clean energy. When do we want it? Now.&rsquo;&rsquo;</p><p>&lsquo;&lsquo;This is important for every single person on the planet, which is why it has to be the greatest grass roots movement of all time,&rsquo;&rsquo; Thompson said. &lsquo;&lsquo;This is the battle of our lives. We&rsquo;re fighting for our children.&rsquo;&rsquo;</p><p>In New York, a contingent came from Moore, Oklahoma, where a massive tornado killed 24 people last year, as did hundreds of people affected by Superstorm Sandy, which the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration and the British meteorological office said was made more likely by climate change.</p><p>In Australia, the largest rally was in Melbourne, where an estimated 10,000 people took to the streets with banners and placards calling on their government to do more to combat global warming.</p><p>Australian Prime Minister Tony Abbott was a particular target of the protesters. Abbott&rsquo;s center-right coalition has removed a carbon tax and has restricted funding for climate change bodies since coming to power last year.</p><p></p><!-- inside story_update.jpt -->
